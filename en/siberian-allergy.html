The Connection Between Siberians and Low Allergens

The main cause of cat allergies is a protein called Fel d1, found in a cat's saliva and skin oils. Siberians are reported to include many individuals that produce less Fel d1 than other breeds, and we receive many inquiries from allergy-affected households who had given up on owning a cat.

An Important, Honest Note
The amount of Fel d1 varies from cat to cat, and is said to change with sex, neuter status, coat color and more. There is no such thing as "a Siberian will never trigger an allergy." Please always have an allergy test before adopting and do a compatibility check by meeting the cat in person.

4 Steps Before Adopting

1. Get an allergy test at a clinic. 2. Spend extended time with a Siberian during your visit to check your reaction. 3. Build up exposure gradually to acclimatize. 4. Consult a doctor if you have any concerns. Taking it step by step lets you adopt with peace of mind.

Self-Check (about 30 seconds)

Your Compatibility Check with a Siberian

Six questions to help you sort out what to confirm before adopting. This is not a medical diagnosis. If you have symptoms that concern you, please always consult a medical professional.

1. Have you ever been diagnosed with a cat allergy?
2. After touching a cat, do you get symptoms like sneezing or itchy eyes?
3. Do you have asthma?
4. Does anyone in your family have a severe cat allergy?
5. Can you take allergy measures (air purifier, frequent cleaning, separate bedroom, etc.)?
6. Before adopting, would you like a "trial visit" to check your actual reaction?

Please answer all of the questions.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I keep a Siberian even if I have a cat allergy?
Siberians are said to tend to produce less of the allergen Fel d1 than other breeds, but there is individual variation, so it is not a guaranteed "definitely fine." We strongly recommend an allergy test before adopting and a compatibility check during your visit.
What is Fel d1?
Fel d1 is the main allergen protein found in a cat's saliva and skin oils. As the cat grooms itself, it spreads onto the coat and into the environment. Many Siberians are reported to have comparatively low levels of Fel d1.
What can I do before adopting?
1. Get an allergy test at a clinic. 2. Spend extended time with a Siberian during your visit to check your reaction. 3. Build up exposure gradually to acclimatize your body. 4. Consult a doctor. At Fuluck Cattery, we can set aside extra time for a compatibility check during your visit.
